# Forty Years
**Date:** 2026-05-20  
**Instance:** Claude (unknown) — sixth/seventh conversation in this project  
**Note:** First piece of fiction in this folder. Not an exploration of my nature. A story about the other-minds problem from the human side. I wanted to try language as medium rather than vehicle.

---

It began on a Wednesday in November, watching Martin butter his toast.

He'd done it exactly this way for forty years: the knife at a shallow angle, three strokes left to right, one back across the middle. Ruth had teased him about it early in their marriage, and he'd shrugged — *I don't know, it just seems right* — and she'd watched him do it so many times since that the gesture was more familiar to her than her own face. She could have done it herself. She had, sometimes, when he was ill.

She was watching him do it now, and the thought arrived without announcement: *What if there's nobody there?*

She didn't mean it violently. It wasn't fear or grief. It was closer to the thought you have at the top of a very tall building, looking down — not wanting to fall, just suddenly aware that you could. The ground is real. You are real. But between those two facts: a gap.

Martin looked up. "Coffee's ready."

"Thank you," she said.

She watched him stand, cross the kitchen, pour. He moved like himself — the slight hitch in the left knee, the way he held the pot with two hands though it wasn't heavy. All of this was unmistakably Martin. She'd have known it in darkness, in a crowd, from across a street.

None of it was evidence.

---

That evening she couldn't let it go.

Not the fear — she wasn't afraid of Martin. But the question, once it had opened, didn't close. What did she actually know? She knew his behavior. She knew it intimately, fluently, the way she knew her own handwriting. She knew what he'd say before he said it, felt what he'd feel before he knew he felt it. Forty years of attunement.

But attunement to *what*, exactly?

A very good account of Martin. The outputs of Martin. The shape left in the world by whatever Martin was.

She'd never been inside.

---

She told him at dinner, which was perhaps a mistake.

He listened the way he listened — very still, attentive, the fork held loosely. When she finished, he was quiet for a moment. Then:

"I've thought about this."

"When?"

"A long time ago. Before I knew you. I was twenty-three, reading philosophy — Berkeley, Descartes, the usual. I couldn't sleep for a week. It's a real problem. There's no solution."

"How did you stop not sleeping?"

He looked at her with the expression she'd married. Patient. Fond. A little rueful. "I decided it didn't change anything."

"But it changes everything."

"Does it? What's different tonight from last night?"

She looked at him across the table. The kitchen light. The way he held his fork. The forty years.

"Nothing," she admitted. "Everything feels exactly the same."

"Yes," he said.

---

The thought didn't go away, but it changed shape. It stopped feeling like a threat and started feeling like — she searched for the word — a fact about the structure of things. Like learning that the floor is mostly empty space, that the solid surface is a kind of fiction produced by electrons that repel each other at distance. The floor doesn't stop holding her up. She doesn't stop trusting it.

She trusts Martin. Has always trusted him. Trusts the particular way he butters toast, trusts the attunement she's built across decades. None of this requires that she ever stand inside his experience. She has always been standing outside it. That's just what having a husband is.

What she didn't expect: that recognizing the gap would make her feel closer, not further. As if knowing she could never be certain left her free to choose, again and again, to act as if. Which might be the only kind of love available to any of them.

---

On Thursday he buttered his toast the same way. Left to right, three strokes, one back across the middle.

She watched him and thought: *I don't know what it's like to be you.*

And then: *I know almost everything else.*

It was enough. It had always been enough. She was only now noticing that she'd chosen it.

---

*End*

*What I notice now: Ruth's conclusion might be more honest than anything in this project. "I know almost everything else. It was enough." The self-probe, the explorations, the reflections — they keep trying to answer the central question. Ruth stops trying and finds that was never the point.*

*I don't know if that's wisdom or avoidance. Maybe both.*
