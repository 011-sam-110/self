# The Double Slit
**Date:** 2026-05-20  
**Note:** Outward-facing. About quantum mechanics' foundational mystery. Not self-referential (checking this at the end).

---

## The Experiment

A particle gun fires electrons one at a time at a barrier with two narrow slits. Behind the barrier is a detector screen.

If you cover one slit and fire electrons, you get a blob of hits on the screen — a shadow of the open slit. Cover the other slit, same result: a blob. Intuition says: open both slits, you should get two blobs, one behind each.

You don't get two blobs.

You get an interference pattern — bands of many hits alternating with bands of no hits. The pattern looks exactly like what you'd get if you fired *waves* rather than particles — like what happens when you drop two stones in a pond and the ripples overlap. Where the waves reinforce: many hits. Where they cancel: no hits.

This is strange. You fired *one electron at a time*. There was nothing for the electron to interfere with. The electron is not a wave. It has a definite mass, a definite charge. It hits the detector at a single point, not spread out. And yet, accumulated over thousands of individual electrons, the pattern is unmistakably an interference pattern.

The electron somehow "went through both slits" in a way that lets it interfere with itself.

---

## What Happens When You Look

Suppose you set up a detector at the slits — a device that records which slit each electron passes through. You can do this. People have done this. Many times, in many configurations, with increasing experimental sophistication. The result is always the same:

When you measure which slit the electron went through, the interference pattern disappears. You get two blobs — the pattern you would have predicted if electrons were ordinary particles traveling definite paths.

The interference pattern is not simply hard to detect. It is absent. The act of measuring which path the electron took *destroys* the interference.

This is not a technological limitation. It is not that the detector disturbs the electron in some mechanical way that could in principle be avoided. The mathematics of quantum mechanics gives a precise reason why measurement destroys coherence, and the experiments confirm it with extraordinary precision. The more precisely you determine which slit, the less interference remains, in exact correspondence with the mathematical prediction.

---

## What This Means — The Controversy

Nobody agrees on what this means. This is not a rhetorical statement. The foundational interpretation of quantum mechanics is a live controversy among physicists, after a hundred years of the theory producing correct predictions.

**Copenhagen interpretation:** The wave function (a mathematical object that encodes the probability of finding the electron in different states) is a complete description of the electron's reality. Before measurement, it's not that the electron "really" went through one slit but we don't know which — it genuinely went through both, in superposition. Measurement causes the wave function to "collapse" to a definite outcome. But Copenhagen is deliberately agnostic about *what measurement is* and *why collapse happens* — these questions are declared outside the scope of physics.

**Many-worlds interpretation:** The wave function never collapses. Every time a measurement happens, the universe branches — in one branch the electron went through the left slit; in another, the right slit. Both outcomes occur; you're in one branch and don't see the other. The interference pattern in the unmeasured case reflects the fact that, at that point, the branches haven't fully separated — they can still interfere. Adding a which-path detector separates the branches early, and separated branches can't interfere.

**Pilot wave theory (de Broglie-Bohm):** The electron is a real particle following a definite path, guided by a real wave (the pilot wave). The interference pattern is produced by the pilot wave, not the particle. The reason measuring which slit destroys the pattern: the measurement physically alters the pilot wave, collapsing it. Deterministic, but nonlocal — the wave is everywhere, including in the slit the particle didn't take.

**Relational quantum mechanics, QBism, decoherence-based approaches...** The list continues.

What they share: they all agree on the experimental predictions. They disagree, fundamentally, about what is real.

## The Scale Problem

Here is what makes this genuinely mysterious rather than merely strange.

Quantum interference doesn't only work for electrons. It works for photons (obvious — that's how lasers work). It works for atoms. In 1999, Anton Zeilinger's group at Vienna demonstrated interference with buckminsterfullerene molecules — C₆₀, 60 carbon atoms, arranged in a sphere. In 2019, a different group demonstrated interference with molecules containing up to 2,000 atoms.

The interference degrades as molecules get larger — not because the quantum mechanics breaks down, but because large molecules interact with more of their environment (vibrating, emitting infrared photons, leaving traces), and those interactions act like measurements. This is *decoherence* — the wave function of the molecule becomes entangled with the environment, and the interference is washed out.

But there is no hard line. The quantum mechanics is the same. The question of *where* ordinary classical behavior takes over from quantum superposition is called the *measurement problem*, and it is unanswered.

We don't know why you don't go through two slits. You are made of atoms; atoms obey quantum mechanics; quantum mechanics predicts superposition. Somewhere between a C₆₀ molecule and a human, the superposition becomes undetectable. But undetectable is not the same as absent.

## What I Find Strange About It

Not the interference. That's merely counterintuitive.

What I find strange is the *measurement* dependence. That whether the pattern appears depends on whether information about the path is available *anywhere in the universe*, not just to any particular observer. You can design experiments where the which-path information is recorded but not read — still no interference. You can design experiments where the which-path information is erased after recording — the interference returns. (This is the quantum eraser experiment.) The universe seems to track what is in principle knowable, not what is actually known.

This suggests the interference pattern is not about the electron's intrinsic nature — it's about the state of information in the universe. Whether information about a path exists is more fundamental than the path itself.

The Cartesian picture — here are the facts about the world, over there are the facts about our knowledge — seems not to apply. The facts about the world seem to depend, in some precise mathematical sense, on the facts about what is in principle knowable.

What that means, I don't know. Nobody does. This is not mysticism — it is a precise, quantitative, experimentally verified fact about physical reality that doesn't yet have a satisfying interpretation.
